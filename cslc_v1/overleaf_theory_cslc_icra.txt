\begin{abstract}
 Contact planning is a fundamental challenge in robotics. It requires both computational speed and physical accuracy. Sphere primitives provide the necessary speed for modern planners. However, modeling contact with spheres is physically inaccurate because point contact cannot capture compliant contact patches or pressure distributions.
 A critical gap exists because no distributed contact model operates natively on spheres. Compliant Sphere Lattice Contact fills this gap. Our method creates a spatially distributed contact patch directly from sphere representations. We show preliminary results demonstrating the recovery of essential contact mechanics.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Physical contact is the fundamental mechanism through which robots interact with their environment. To accomplish tasks like washing dishes or clearing a table, robots must accurately predict how objects will respond to contact. Model-based contact formulations enable these predictions for simulation, trajectory optimization, planning, and control \cite{le2024contact}. However, applying these models in real time introduces a challenging computational tradeoff. Contact models must capture complex physical interactions while remaining lightweight enough to evaluate inside tight control loops.

Modern robotics increasingly utilizes sphere primitives as a primary geometric representation to satisfy the computational demands of planning in real time. Motion planning frameworks such as cuRobo \cite{sundaralingam2023curobo, sundaralingam2026curobov2}, VAMP \cite{thomason2024motions}, and AkinoPDF \cite{duong2026ultrafast} represent robot links as collections of spheres because they enable exceptionally fast collision checking and admit analytical signed distance queries. Furthermore, contact planners adopt spheres because their geometry is fully differentiable \cite{kurtz2026inverse, pang2023global}. However, the contact models applied to these sphere representations sacrifice physical accuracy. The standard approach relies on point contact. Point contact inherently lacks a contact patch area, meaning it cannot model rotational stiffness, frictional moments, or pressure distributions \cite{masterjohn2022velocity, elandt2019pressure}. When a robot grasps a flat book or a curved cylinder, a point contact model localizes the entire reaction force at a single infinitely small location. This creates fragile grasps that are highly sensitive to microscopic pose errors. To compensate for this instability, planners are forced to artificially inflate friction coefficients or command excessive grip forces. These workarounds inevitably suppress the nuanced mechanics required for dexterous manipulation and risk damaging fragile objects.

We introduce Compliant Sphere Lattice Contact (CSLC) to fill this gap. CSLC is a distributed contact model that unifies the speed and differentiability of sphere primitives with the physical fidelity previously available only to methods based on meshes. Rather than relying on rigid points or volumetric intersections, CSLC models the robot interface as a compliant lattice. The surface consists of sphere primitives connected by anchor springs and lateral springs. When pressed against an object, these spheres displace to form a spatially distributed contact patch. This formulation recovers rotational stiffness, continuous pressure gradients, and accurate frictional moments directly from the spheres already present in the collision pipeline.

The proposed model is differentiable everywhere and parallelizable across multiple contacts. It solves to quasistatic equilibrium efficiently at each timestep to satisfy the strict computational budgets of modern control architectures. In summary, our main contributions are: (1) a novel distributed contact model formulated entirely using sphere primitives, (2) a parallelizable solver tailored for optimization in real time, and (3) preliminary experimental validation demonstrating the recovery of patch area mechanics and rotational stiffness in manipulation tasks.

This work
is our initial step toward the broader goal of creating a contact model that is differentiable, physically informative, and practical for real-time robotics. These properties are essential for model-based manipulation and learning-based methods. The  performance of these methods are directly tied to the strengths and limitations of the underlying contact models \cite{yang2025physics, kanehira2025rl}.

\section{Related Work}
Most robotics simulators rely on rigid or locally compliant point‑contact models (Linear Complementarity Problem (LCP), Nonlinear Complementarity Problem (NCP), or Cone Complementarity Problem (CCP) variants with Hunt–Crossley‑style normal laws), which regularize force–penetration but still concentrate load at isolated points and therefore cannot represent contact patch area, pressure distributions, or rotational stiffness \cite{le2024contact, drake, todorov2012mujoco, coumans2019, liang2018gpu}. Distributed patch‑based models such as Pressure Field Contact (PFC) and its hydroelastic and discrete variants address these limitations by precomputing pressure fields over volumetric or surface meshes and integrating across a contact surface \cite{elandt2019pressure,masterjohn2022velocity,Cas21}. These require volumetric or surface meshes plus offline pressure‑field precomputation.

Surface-contact rigid-body methods model extended rigid patches and their equivalent wrenches \cite{Xie16,Xie18}, whereas soft-finger models encode compliance in a local lumped contact law \cite{Xyd99,Cio06,Xu19,Bak14}. CSLC differs from both by representing the interface as a compliant distributed sphere lattice, so patch formation and load sharing emerge directly on sphere-based geometric surfaces.

Complementarity‑free and contact‑implicit formulations instead focus on how to integrate contact into optimization and control, reformulating time‑stepping to remove explicit complementarity constraints and enabling real‑time dexterous manipulation, but they still assume either point‑based or mesh‑based contact laws and do not tackle distributed contact in sphere‑based representations \cite{jin2024complementarity, sleiman2019contact, doshi2019contact, kurtz2022contact}.

In contrast to these existing approaches, our method achieves the physical fidelity of distributed pressure fields while maintaining the speed and differentiability of sphere-based representations. In the next section, we detail how our method works by introducing the sphere lattice, formulating the underlying distributed forces, and demonstrating how the system reaches quasistatic equilibrium.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview}

We first approximate the robot and object geometries as collections of spheres using MorphIt \cite{nechyporenko2025morphit}. While these objects obey standard rigid body dynamics, their surface spheres interact through our Compliant Sphere Lattice Contact (CSLC) model (see \ref{fig:diagram}). By connecting the spheres with a network of springs to simulate a deformable skin, CSLC distributes interaction forces to form a continuous contact patch rather than an isolated point contact.

\subsection{Sphere Lattice Kinematics}


We start with an arbitrary rigid body that has a defined position and
orientation. A collection of surface spheres covers this body. Each
sphere $i$ has a rest position $\mathbf{p}_i$ fixed to the underlying
rigid body, a rest radius $r_i$, and a precomputed outward unit normal
$\hat{\mathbf{n}}_i$ determined by its position on the body surface.
When the body interacts with another object, the compliant skin at
sphere~$i$ compresses inward along its normal by a scalar amount
$\delta_i \geq 0$. We model this compression as a reduction of the
sphere's effective contact radius while the sphere centre
$\mathbf{p}_i$ remains locked to the underlying rigid body:
\begin{equation}
    r_i^{\text{eff}} = r_i - \delta_i,
    \qquad \delta_i \in [0,\,r_i].
    \label{eq:sphere_pos}
\end{equation}
Keeping the centre fixed and retreating only the contact surface is
equivalent to a linear penalty model of a compliant layer: the bulk
rigid-body kinematics are unchanged, and only the local contact surface
participates in the quasistatic relaxation. This choice yields a stable
series-spring coupling between the anchor and contact springs
(\cref{sec:solver}) without constraining $k_a$ relative to $k_c$.

This normal-projection formulation exploits the structure of quasistatic
contact: the dominant deformation mode is compression along the surface
normal, while tangential load redistribution is captured by the lateral
coupling (\cref{eq:lateral}) and tangential friction is resolved by the
rigid-body solver (\cref{eq:friction}). Restricting the unknown to a
scalar reduces the system dimension from $3n$ to~$n$, yielding a
$3{\times}$ reduction in memory and iteration cost for the equilibrium
solver while preserving the patch geometry that governs rotational
stiffness and pressure distribution.

We stack the scalar compressions of all $n$ surface spheres into a single
vector $\boldsymbol{\delta} = [\delta_1, \ldots, \delta_n]^\top \in
\mathbb{R}^n$.


\subsection{Forces on Each Sphere}

Three distinct forces act on every surface sphere $i$. These forces determine how the body deforms during physical contact.

\subsubsection{Anchor Spring}
A linear spring connects each sphere to its rest position. This spring resists the displacement of the sphere. The anchor force is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_i^{\text{anchor}} = -k_a\,\boldsymbol{\delta}_i,
    \label{eq:anchor}
\end{equation}
where $k_a > 0$ represents the anchor stiffness.

\subsubsection{Lateral Coupling}
Lateral springs connect adjacent spheres to each other. These connections spread the contact load across a larger area instead of keeping it concentrated at a single point. The lateral force on sphere $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_i^{\text{lateral}}
        = -k_\ell \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)}
          \bigl(\boldsymbol{\delta}_i - \boldsymbol{\delta}_j\bigr),
    \label{eq:lateral}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}(i)$ represents the neighboring spheres and $k_\ell \geq 0$ controls the load spreading.

In matrix form, the lateral force on all spheres is
$\mathbf{f}^{\text{lateral}} = -k_\ell\,L\,\boldsymbol{\delta}$,
where $L \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is the discrete graph Laplacian
of the sphere adjacency graph. The Kronecker product with $I_3$ that would
appear in the full vector formulation is unnecessary because the
normal-projection reduces each displacement to a scalar.


The lateral spring coupling plays the role of a continuous pressure field as in the hydroelastic model \cite{masterjohn2022velocity}. Both mechanisms ensure that the contact load is distributed across the patch rather than concentrated at a single point.

\subsubsection{Contact Force}
When sphere $i$ interacts with a sphere $j$ on another object, we compute
a signed penetration depth that accounts for the scalar compression
(\cref{eq:sphere_pos}). The rest-geometry overlap between the two spheres
is
\begin{equation}
    \phi_{ij}^{\text{rest}}
        = (r_i + r_j) - \|\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j^{\text{obj}}\|,
    \label{eq:pen_rest}
\end{equation}
and the effective penetration that drives the contact force is obtained
by subtracting the skin compression $\delta_i$ from the rest overlap:
\begin{equation}
    \phi_{ij}
        = \bigl[\phi_{ij}^{\text{rest}} - \delta_i\bigr]_+
        = \bigl[(r_i - \delta_i + r_j)
                 - \|\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{p}_j^{\text{obj}}\|\bigr]_+,
    \label{eq:pen}
\end{equation}
where $[\cdot]_+ = \max(\cdot,0)$. Contact is active when
$\phi_{ij} > 0$. Writing the effective penetration in this form makes
explicit that the compliant skin acts in series with the contact
penalty: as $\delta_i$ grows, $\phi_{ij}$ decays toward zero, giving a
stable series response between the anchor and contact springs.

The normal force follows the Hunt--Crossley model
\cite{hunt1975coefficient}, which ensures the force smoothly goes to zero
when penetration ends:
\begin{equation}
    f_{n,ij} = k_c\,\phi_{ij}\,
               \bigl(1 + d_c\,[-v_n]_+\bigr)_+,
    \label{eq:normal}
\end{equation}
where $k_c$ is the contact stiffness, $d_c$ is the damping coefficient,
and $v_n$ is the normal approach velocity. The quasistatic lattice
equilibrium is solved using only the static penalty component
$k_c\,\phi_{ij}$; the velocity-dependent damping is forwarded to the
rigid-body dynamics solver. When the rigid-body solver supports a
native Hunt--Crossley law (e.g.\ a semi-implicit penalty integrator) the
damping acts on macroscopic motion. When the rigid-body solver is
constraint-based (e.g.\ MuJoCo), $d_c$ has no direct analogue in the
constraint-relaxation framework and the corresponding constraint is
parametrised by $k_c$ alone using a critically damped time constant.


Finally, we calculate tangential friction using a regularized Coulomb model \cite{Cas23}. This formulation makes the friction force continuously differentiable everywhere. The friction force is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_{t,ij} = -\mu\,f_{n,ij}
        \frac{\mathbf{v}_{t,ij}}{\|\mathbf{v}_{t,ij}\| + \varepsilon},
    \label{eq:friction}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the friction coefficient and $\varepsilon > 0$ prevents sudden jumps when the slipping stops.

\subsection{Lattice Stiffness Matrix}

We combine the anchor and lateral spring forces into a single internal restoring force.

This combined force projected onto the normal direction gives the
scalar restoring force $f_i^{\text{int}} = -[\mathbf{K}\boldsymbol{\delta}]_i$.
The matrix $\mathbf{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is the lattice stiffness
matrix with entries:


\begin{equation}
    K_{ii} = k_a + k_\ell\,|\mathcal{N}(i)|,
    \quad
    K_{ij} = -k_\ell \;\text{ if } j \in \mathcal{N}(i),
    \label{eq:K}
\end{equation}
and zero otherwise. Because the displacement is scalar,
$\mathbf{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is a standard scalar matrix
rather than a $3n {\times} 3n$ block system.
The matrix is sparse, symmetric, and positive definite when $k_a > 0$,
and depends only on the lattice topology and spring constants,
so it is assembled and factored once at initialization.

To ensure the model is easily tunable, we automatically calibrate the microscopic per-sphere contact stiffness, $k_c$, from a macroscopic user-defined bulk stiffness, $k_e^{\text{bulk}}$. Assuming a uniform flat contact where the Laplacian vanishes (equal displacements), the effective stiffness per sphere balances as $N_{\text{contact}} (k_c k_a) / (k_a + k_c) = k_e^{\text{bulk}}$. This derivation is inverted during initialization to solve for $k_c$, allowing users to specify real-world material properties without tuning individual discrete sphere parameters.
\subsection{Quasistatic Equilibrium Solver} \label{sec:solver}

We compute the sphere compressions at each timestep by balancing the
forces on every sphere $i$. The anchor, lateral, and contact forces must
sum to zero:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_i^{\text{anchor}}
    + \mathbf{f}_i^{\text{lateral}}
    + \mathbf{f}_i^{\text{contact}}
    = \mathbf{0}.
    \label{eq:qstatic_expand}
\end{equation}

Substituting the per-sphere forces
(\cref{eq:anchor,eq:lateral,eq:pen,eq:normal}) and grouping the internal
forces into the lattice stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ (\cref{eq:K}) yields
the compact system
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{K}\,\boldsymbol{\delta}
        = k_c\,\bigl[\boldsymbol{\phi}^{\text{rest}}(\mathbf{q}_{\text{body}})
          - \boldsymbol{\delta}\bigr]_+,
    \label{eq:qstatic}
\end{equation}
which expands, per sphere, to
\begin{equation}
    \underbrace{k_a\,\delta_i
        + k_\ell \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)}
          (\delta_i - \delta_j)}_{[\mathbf{K}\boldsymbol{\delta}]_i}
    \;=\;
    \underbrace{k_c\,\bigl[\phi_i^{\text{rest}} - \delta_i\bigr]_+}_{\text{normal contact force}}.
    \label{eq:qstatic_full}
\end{equation}
The right-hand side is the reduced contact penalty
(\cref{eq:pen,eq:normal}) and captures the series coupling between the
soft anchor spring and the stiff contact spring: as $\delta_i$ grows, the
effective penetration $\phi_i^{\text{rest}} - \delta_i$ shrinks, and the
contact force decays until it balances the anchor restoring force. For a
single isolated sphere with $k_\ell = 0$, the equilibrium reduces to
$\delta_i^* = k_c\,\phi_i^{\text{rest}}/(k_a + k_c)$ and the corresponding
contact force is
$f_{n,i}^* = k_c k_a\,\phi_i^{\text{rest}}/(k_a + k_c)$, giving the
series effective stiffness
$k_{\text{eff}} = k_c k_a /(k_a + k_c)$ used in the calibration of
\cref{eq:K}. Because $\mathbf{K}$ is positive definite for $k_a > 0$ and
the augmented active-contact system
$\mathbf{K} + k_c\,\mathbf{I}_{\text{active}}$ is diagonally dominant,
the quasistatic solve is unconditionally stable.

Because the equilibrium is projected onto the surface normal, only the
normal component of the contact force enters the solve. Tangential
friction forces are computed from the resulting normal-force distribution
and applied to the rigid body by the dynamics solver.

The right-hand side is nonlinear in $\boldsymbol{\delta}$ through the
$[\cdot]_+$ clamp. We solve the system using damped Jacobi iteration,
warm-started with the compression from the previous timestep. The
iteration absorbs the active-contact diagonal term $+k_c$ into the
relaxation for every sphere whose effective penetration is positive, so
the implicit system converges reliably.

This approach is highly parallelisable. Contact forces for every sphere
are computed independently, and damped Jacobi updates all compressions
simultaneously rather than sequentially, mapping naturally to GPU
hardware so that the solver can meet the strict timing requirements of
real-time control.

\subsection{Rigid Body Dynamics}

The physical interaction produces a net force and torque on the interacting rigid bodies. According to Newton's third law, the bodies experience equal and opposite contact wrenches. The total force and torque on a body are:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{F}^{\text{body}} &= -\sum_{i,j} \mathbf{f}_{ij}, \\
    \boldsymbol{\tau}^{\text{body}} &=
        \sum_{i,j}
        \bigl(\mathbf{c}_{ij} - \mathbf{x}_b\bigr)
        \times (-\mathbf{f}_{ij}),
    \label{eq:wrench}
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{c}_{ij}$ is the contact point and $\mathbf{x}_b$ is the center of mass of the body. We update the state of the rigid body over time using semi implicit Euler integration.

\section{Experiments, Results \& Discussion}

All experiments use a custom rigid body simulator with semi-implicit Euler
integration. The baseline point contact model applies Hunt\textendash Crossley
normal forces (\cref{eq:normal}) and regularized Coulomb friction
(\cref{eq:friction}) at a single sphere. CSLC uses the same per-sphere force
laws but distributes them across a lattice pad and solves for quasistatic
equilibrium at each timestep. Both models share identical stiffness, damping,
and friction parameters so that all differences arise solely from the contact
representation.

\subsection{Lattice Deflection Under Point Load}

We first verify that the spring network distributes load as intended before
coupling it to rigid body dynamics. A 1.0\,N normal force is applied to the
centre sphere of a $15{\times}15$ lattice pad and we solve
$\mathbf{K}\boldsymbol{\delta} = \mathbf{f}$ directly. Without lateral
coupling ($k_\ell = 0$), only the loaded sphere deflects. As $k_\ell$
increases, the deformation spreads radially, broadening the dimple and
reducing peak displacement (\cref{fig:deflection}). This confirms that the
graph Laplacian coupling converts a concentrated point load into a spatially
distributed contact patch.

\subsection{Rotational Grasp Stability}

A 1.2\,kg rectangular object ($16{\times}300{\times}400$\,mm) is gripped
symmetrically on its flat covers. Each finger is modeled as an
$11{\times}11$ CSLC lattice pad with 0.4\,mm nominal penetration,
$\mu = 0.3$, and $d_c = 2.0$\,s/m. At $t{=}0$ the object receives an
impulsive angular velocity perturbation.

Under a tilt perturbation ($\omega_y = 3.0$\,rad/s), the point contact model
acts as a frictionless pivot because the single contact point lies on the
rotation axis and generates no restoring moment (\cref{fig:diagram}). CSLC
produces a pressure gradient across the patch that generates a restoring
torque and damps the oscillation within approximately 200\,ms. Under a twist
perturbation ($\omega_x = 3.0$\,rad/s), point contact again produces zero
torsional moment. CSLC distributes friction across the full patch area, and
each sphere contributes a moment proportional to its distance from the patch
centre, rapidly stabilizing the object (\cref{fig:twist}). These two
perturbation axes confirm that CSLC recovers both bending and torsional
stiffness that point contact cannot provide.

\subsection{Friction Limited Weight Capacity}

We hold the object vertically under gravity with no angular perturbation,
incrementing its mass from 50\,g to 2.5\,kg at a fixed grip penetration of
1.0\,mm and $\mu = 0.5$. The grip must support the full weight through
friction alone. Because CSLC distributes the grip across 121 lattice spheres,
its aggregate friction capacity exceeds that of a single point contact at the
same penetration. The point contact model exceeds its friction limit at
approximately 200\,g and slips progressively as mass increases. CSLC holds
objects up to 2.5\,kg with sub-millimetre displacement (\cref{fig:weight}).

\subsection{Discussion}

Across all three experiments, CSLC recovers contact mechanics that point
contact cannot represent: continuous pressure gradients, rotational stiffness,
and distributed friction capacity. These properties emerge directly from the
lattice topology and require no mesh preprocessing or volumetric pressure
field computation. However, all experiments are simulation only and restricted
to controlled, near-planar contact geometries. Hardware validation, irregular
object shapes, and multi-finger coordination are necessary next steps.

\section{Limitations}
CSLC assumes small, quasi-static deformations and isotropic material behavior, shared assumptions with PFC and the hydroelastic contact model. Neither Poisson coupling, anisotropic stiffness, nor large-deformation effects such as buckling are captured; full finite element treatment remains necessary for such cases.

The scalar normal-projection formulation is exact for planar contact patches
where all surface normals are parallel, and introduces negligible error for
near-planar patches typical of fingertip contact. For highly curved surfaces
where neighboring normals diverge significantly, the full vector formulation
$\boldsymbol{\delta}_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$ would capture cross-normal shear
coupling at the cost of tripling the solver dimension.


Resolution changes require rescaling contact stiffness and calibrating $k_\ell/k_a$ to measured material properties \cite{kumar2015viscoelastic}. Finally, the quasistatic solver is valid only when lattice relaxation is fast relative to rigid-body motion, breaking down during high-speed impacts.

\section{Future Work \& Conclusion}
Immediate next steps include implementing a GPU-parallel solver, integrating the model into a model predictive control (MPC) loop, and enabling end-to-end differentiability via JAX or PyTorch for gradient-based trajectory optimization and policy learning. We plan to integrate CSLC into modern simulators such as Drake to directly benchmark against the hydroelastic contact model. Additionally, we aim to incorporate CSLC into Newton to leverage hardware acceleration; this will facilitate principled comparisons against existing models and provide open, user-friendly access for the broader research community. Accurately modeling the gripper–object interface is essential for dexterous manipulation and for data-driven policies that rely on the physical fidelity of their underlying simulators. Ultimately, CSLC represents a principled step toward a contact model that is simultaneously fast, differentiable, and physically informative.
